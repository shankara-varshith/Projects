The three leakage patterns that show up everywhere

After analysing hundreds of transactions across different profiles, the same patterns keep surfacing:

  • 1
    Duplicate and ghost charges Subscriptions billed twice, platforms charging after cancellation, recurring fees for services never used. These are small individually — ₹200 here, ₹500 there — but they compound to significant annual losses that nobody catches because each charge is too small to notice.
  • 2
    Failed transaction retries A payment fails. The user retries. The merchant charges a fee. The original also processes later. For businesses processing hundreds of transactions daily, this is a structural drain — not a one-off. Our analysis at FairMoney showed this alone was costing merchants millions monthly.
  • 3
    Uncategorised small-value spend Dozens of sub-₹500 UPI transactions to unnamed merchants. Individually harmless. Collectively, they often represent the largest leakage category — particularly for MSMEs who pay for micro-tools, delivery fees, and ad-hoc services without ever tracking them.

The FinTech opportunity nobody is talking about

Here's where it gets interesting for the industry.

Every bank and FinTech already holds this transaction data. They have it for millions of customers. But almost none of them are using it to proactively surface financial insights to those customers — they're using it for risk scoring, fraud detection, and compliance. That's it.

The opportunity is to flip the model: instead of using transaction data on customers, use it for them.

How FinTechs can use this intelligence

Transaction pattern analysis isn't just about finding leakage. It's a window into a customer's entire financial behaviour — their pain points, their spending categories, their financial maturity. That's a cross-sell and upsell map.

A customer with high failed transaction retries → offer a transaction retry management feature or a float facility.

A merchant with settlement delay patterns → offer early settlement or a working capital loan. They already have a demonstrated need.

An individual with growing uncategorised spend → offer a budgeting product, a spend management card, or a savings goal feature.

The insight is already there. The question is whether the product team acts on it.

This is what I mean when I say leakage analysis is a distribution strategy, not just a financial tool. The moment you show a customer "you lost ₹28,000 last year to these five patterns" — and then offer them a product that solves it — the conversion writes itself.

What building this taught me about AI products

I built this in five days using vibe coding — no backend team, no designer, no data scientist. Just an idea, a prompt, and a deployment pipeline. But the most important technical decision wasn't the stack. It was the AI prompt.

Early versions returned findings that sounded authoritative but were useless — minor one-off anomalies dressed up as patterns. The model was being helpful in the wrong way, filling its output slots with whatever it could find rather than what actually mattered.

The fix was specific: define what "major" means before asking the model to find it. A pattern costing less than 1–2% of total transaction volume isn't worth reporting. One-off anomalies are noise, not signal. Evidence must quote specific transaction names, dates, and amounts — not summaries.

"In AI products, the prompt is the product. Everything else — the UI, the loading screen, the database — exists to deliver the prompt's output in the most useful way possible."

This is the lesson I'd carry into any AI PM role: the intelligence layer determines whether the product is useful or just impressive. Getting that right requires domain knowledge, not just engineering skill. You have to know what a good answer looks like before you can prompt for it.
